Why I Left The Faith, and Why It Wasn’t For Me

Written by: Andrew Augustine

I figured since I’ve made this decision, and I’m probably gonna be asked several times why, and probably have to have a bunch of conversations about it, so I figured I should just wrap it all up into a paper. In short, I just personally don't believe that the faith is for me. It's too questionable, with flaws in logic, and shoddy attempts at coming up with excuses to cover them up. And they just have to have an answer, even if it's a cheap one, for everything. As a man of logic, it just didn't work for me.

And you can't forget that the faith over complicates everything, and this is personified best in none other than catholic religious education. There are some definitions that are totally contradictory to their english language counterparts, or have nothing to do with the original words. Like, Salvation History isn't defined as actual history, rather as the exact opposite, a story, that was made to explain our creation and saving from sin.

Now don't get me wrong, this paper isn't meant to convince you guys to leave the faith. Likewise, it's not just gonna be a royal diss track for why the faith sucks, because honestly it doesn't. The catholic faith, over the two-thousand years of its existence, has done some wonderful things for humanity. The creation of institutions to teach others morals, getting people to meet each other, creating friendships that last a lifetime; It's done some really good things. Just... being a member of a catholic school where they shove bullshit answers down your throat for literally everything, and then relate everything back to the faith, it didn't work for me.

Praying isn't gonna make the terminally ill better. It's not gonna cure cancer. It's not gonna end the unruly reign of dictators and immoral people. What will do all that, however, is action. Doing something to actually combat these forces of evil, will make change however. Actively giving time or money to support those in need will make change. Creating tools to help other unfortunate people will make change. Standing up to the powers that opress will make change. Some otherworldly figure isn't gonna come down and make everything better. We are the change. Praying instead of doing is just another excuse.

Here's my most convincing argument to date, and the ultimate reason I left. As a man of logic, I look at the outside world seeking information that justifies why things are the way that they are. Why they do what they do. This study, which I ultimately found all my decision making upon, is called science. Every time a statement comes out in politics, I ask for the science and the facts behind it. In everything that I do, I look for the science to back it up.

It is this drive, this curiosity, that brought me to one topic that changed my entire view of religion: Thanatophobia. The fear of death itself. Every human who has ever lived had this. My belief is that the "almighty loving god" we all know and love, is a mental method created to cope with this phobia. God is simply human imagination that went viral, so to speak. Early humans were scared of what came after life once they saw their friends and family die around them, so they created the idea of the afterlife. And a god to rule it all. They also didn't understand the science behind reproduction, and likewise death, so they just assumed that it was a greater being doing it all. Every early civilization did it. From Eurasia, to South America, to Africa; All cultures thought of this. Our Christian god is no different from any of the others.

But that brings up another point that I find most interesting. If we created gods as we grew in civilization, who's to say that other creatures couldn't have as well? The human's natural first-line response to death, or anything that threatens to bring us closer to that nonexistence, is called "Fight or Flight." Whenever we feel threatened, we either respond by fighting the forces that threaten us, or by running away. However, it's proven that we aren't the only creatures with this response. For example, if you try hitting a dog with a bat, they're either gonna do one of two things: fight the bat that threatens it, or try to get the hell outta dodge. If they're scared of things hurting them and killing them, then by definition, they have thanataphobia as well. And if the creation of gods came about from our civilizations advancing and fearing thanataphobia, then who's to say that other smart creatures, like dolphins, who are known to communicate, haven't come up with their own god as well? If that is true, then that raises another question: What makes us different from other creatures? And that answer's simple:

Nothing. We're just the dominant race because of three simple reasons: First, we were the fastest to evolve, and in that, we were uncontested. We're the only creatures that have evolved fast enough to create civilizations and networks as advanced as ours. If we look forward in a billion years, I'll bet any amount of money that humans won't be the only dominant species on planet earth. At that point there will be other species that caught up to us in the evolutionary race, that are just as advanced as we are today, and we'll have to share the planet with them.

Second: We have written language. The creation of paper and pen, written knowledge has allowed us to share ideas and thoughts better than any other species on this planet. It's allowed us to share great works, with amazing ideas, and communicate ideas better than any civilization without it.

Third: Our curiosity. Our constant drive to figure out why things are the way they are drove us further in our quest for knowledge than the others. Why do we exist? Why do things fall to the ground? Why this? Why that? This curiosity motivates us, even to this day. And ultimately, it is this curiosity that brought me to this realization.

So, do I believe god exists? The answer is, surprisingly, yes. But I do not believe that he is this otherworldly figure that can break all known laws of physics, thermodynamics, et cetera, because he feels like it. I believe that god is an idea created by humans that helps us make greater decisions, and help our civilization grow. And I fully support this idea.

But now you may be asking "Hey Andrew, what about Jesus Christ, the son of god, who came down, died for our sins, and three days later was resurrected, huh? How about that?" Well, I can contest that as well. One of the things that pestered me about the catholic faith was just sheer coincidence. Say it's a rainy day, and someone's having a bad day. When it stops raining and a rainbow shows up, is it "a message from god that everything's gonna be okay?" No, it's literally just because the rain stopped, and there's water particles in the air that diffuse the light and make a "rainbow" appear. Sheer coincidence.

I believe that the whole idea of Jesus is the same. A bunch of coincidences that matched up to godly proportions. (Literally.) A man, who believed he was the son of god, went around preaching and was ultimately killed. Well... maybe he wasn't killed. It's showed that the body can enter extreme situations in order to survive. Maybe Jesus's body, when he "breathed his last" just entered shock or a deep coma, and when he was taken down and bandaged up, was given the proper necessities to heal. Maybe he never died at all. (Admittedly, this part of the paper is a little bit more sketchy than the rest but still.) And I can get into the whole Roman documents deal, but I'll spare the boring parts. TL;DR: They say that he existed, but don't acknowledge his works at all. Therefore we have no proof, and the bible can't be referenced as a valid source since it was written by a bunch of Jesus's followers.

Now don't get me wrong, this non-belief in Jesus and god isn't me saying "Ha screw morals, I'm gonna go do drugs, have unprecedented amounts of sex, kill a bunch of people, and do everything morally incorrect as I can." Because as fucked up as I am, I still have morals. Not "catholic morality" or whatever that stupid-ass definition is, just good old-fashioned "Is this helping or hurting people?" (with some add-ons, of course.) Does this ultimately make me happy? Not like, temporary pleasure, but does this make me truly happy? Is this fulfilling? Helping that man on the side of the street, does that make me happy? Leading my friends on their computer careers? Supporting all my friends who are faith-filled to experience their true happiness? The answer is yes. Because humans were created to help one another, and to lead one another. This is what makes me happy.

Now, here's another important side-point. This paper is not for rebellious high-schoolers to use and say "Yeah I believe in that" as an excuse to go do immoral things in college, because ultimately, unless you can prove that you believe in something else, and write up a however-many-pages-long-this-paper-is paper, you honestly aren't in the morally right to make a decision like that. And that's actually a good segue.

What I now believe in, is good and evil. And most humans, over many faiths, believe in this as well. Most are personified in the forms of God and Satan, or Yin and Yang, but I just believe in the ideas of good and evil themselves. Not personified in any way. Just general good, and general evil. And to guide me is my good old-fashioned morals. It's taken me through all of middle-school and high-school, and it hasn't led me wrong yet, in leadership and not in leadership, in social life and not in social life, so I'm just gonna stick with it.

I guess I'll wrap up with this. Once again, I'm not dissing the Catholic (or I guess really, Christian) faith. These are just the reasons I believe in that caused me to leave. If you want, you can believe that everything I just said is bullshit, and I get it. I support you all through every decision you guys make. If you're gay, straight, trans or not, catholic or not, I support everyone's decisions. As long as it makes you happy, and you fully believe in it, I in turn, fully support you and that decision. And I hope you all can come to do the same with me.
